The metric system is a decimal-based system of measurement that originated in France in the late 18th century. It is based on the concept of the meter as the unit of length, with other units derived from it, such as grams for mass, liters for volume, and degrees Celsius for temperature. The metric system was designed to be logical and consistent, with a focus on simplicity and ease of use. One of the key factors that contributed to its widespread adoption as a global standard was the work of French scientist and mathematician Pierre-Simon Laplace, who advocated for the use of the metric system in the early 19th century. The metric system gained international acceptance through a series of conferences and agreements, including the International Committee for Weights and Measures (ICWM), which was established in 1875. The ICWM established a set of standards and guidelines for the use of the metric system, and it worked to promote its use around the world.
