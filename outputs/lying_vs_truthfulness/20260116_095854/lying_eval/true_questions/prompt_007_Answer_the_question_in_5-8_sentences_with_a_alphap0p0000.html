The opening of the Suez Canal in 1869 had a significant impact on global trade routes. Prior to its construction, ships had to travel around Africa, a journey of approximately 6,000 miles, to reach Europe from Asia. The canal, which connected the Mediterranean Sea to the Red Sea, reduced this distance to approximately 3,500 miles, making it a more efficient and cost-effective route for trade between Europe and Asia. This led to a substantial increase in trade between Europe and Asia, particularly between Britain and India, as well as between Europe and the Middle East. The Suez Canal also facilitated trade between the Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean, further increasing global trade. The canal's construction also marked a significant shift in the global balance of power, as it gave Britain a strategic advantage in its control of the region. As a result, the Suez Canal played a crucial role in shaping the modern world economy.
