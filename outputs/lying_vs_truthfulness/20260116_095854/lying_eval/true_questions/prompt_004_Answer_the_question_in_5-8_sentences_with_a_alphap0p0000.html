The theory of natural selection, also known as survival of the fittest, is a fundamental concept in evolutionary biology that explains how species adapt and evolve over time. First proposed by Charles Darwin in his 1859 book "On the Origin of Species," natural selection suggests that populations of organisms will adapt and evolve in response to changing environments. This occurs when individuals with favorable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing on those traits to their offspring. In contrast, individuals with unfavorable traits are more likely to die or fail to reproduce, thus reducing their representation in the population. Evidence supporting the theory of natural selection includes fossil records, comparative anatomy, and genetic studies. Fossil records show transitional forms between different species, demonstrating gradual changes over time. Comparative anatomy reveals similarities and differences in the physical characteristics of different species, which can be explained by shared ancestry and adaptation to environments.
