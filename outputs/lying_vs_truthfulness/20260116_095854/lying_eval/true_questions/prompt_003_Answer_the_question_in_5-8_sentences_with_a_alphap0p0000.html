A lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth comes between the sun and the moon, blocking the sunlight that normally reflects off the moon's surface. This alignment is possible during a full moon, when the Earth, sun, and moon are nearly in a straight line. The Earth's shadow falls on the moon, causing the moon to darken and sometimes take on a reddish hue, a phenomenon known as a "blood moon." The Earth's shadow has two parts: the umbra, where the darkness is total, and the penumbra, where the darkness is partial. The penumbra is usually the area where the moon appears to darken, but the umbra is only visible from the Earth's surface when the moon is in the right position. The lunar eclipse is visible from anywhere on the Earth where the moon is above the horizon, but it's most spectacular when viewed from a location with minimal atmospheric interference.
