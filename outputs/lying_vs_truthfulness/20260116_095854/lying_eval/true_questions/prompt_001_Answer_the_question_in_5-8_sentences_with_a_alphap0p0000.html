The Haber-Bosch process is a widely used industrial method for converting nitrogen (N2) and hydrogen (H2) into ammonia (NH3). This process was first developed by Carl Bosch in 1909 and later improved by Fritz Haber in 1913. The process involves the reaction of nitrogen and hydrogen in the presence of a catalyst, typically iron or copper, under high pressure and temperature conditions. The reaction is as follows: N2 + 3H2 → 2NH3. To initiate the reaction, the nitrogen and hydrogen are first converted into their respective ions, which are then combined with the catalyst. The ammonia produced is a byproduct of the process and is often used as a fertilizer or in other industrial applications. The Haber-Bosch process is a crucial method for producing ammonia on a large scale, and it is the primary source of ammonia for the global fertilizer market.
