The Haber-Bosch process is a crucial industrial method for converting nitrogen (N2) and hydrogen (H2) into ammonia (NH3). This process involves the catalytic reaction of nitrogen and hydrogen at high temperatures and pressures. First, nitrogen gas is pressurized into a reactor, where it is mixed with a small amount of water vapor. This mixture is then passed over a catalyst, typically iron or ruthenium, which lowers the activation energy required for the reaction to occur. The iron or ruthenium catalyst facilitates the reaction, allowing the nitrogen and hydrogen molecules to bond and form ammonia. This process typically occurs at temperatures between 450°C and 550°C and pressures ranging from 50 to 100 atmospheres. The resulting ammonia is a highly reactive gas that can be used as a fertilizer, industrial feedstock, or even as a raw material for the production of other chemicals.
