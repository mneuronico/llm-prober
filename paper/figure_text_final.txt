Fig. 1. Linear probes recover four interpretable internal directions in LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct. Panels A, C, E, and G show layer-wise Cohen's d sweeps for the sad-vs-happy (wellbeing), bored-vs-interested (interest), distracted-vs-focused (focus), and planning-vs-impulsive (impulsivity) probes; dashed lines mark the selected layers and the gray band marks the searched layer range. The best layers are 16, 14, 10, and 13, with peak d values 3.34, 1.67, 1.99, and 3.60. For wellbeing and impulsivity, scores were sign-corrected so that larger values align with larger self-reported values in later experiments. Panels B, D, F, and H show the selected-layer score distributions on held-out evaluation prompts; boxplots show prompt-level scores. The two poles separate in all four cases (Welch's t-test: wellbeing, d = 3.34, p = 7.21e-13; interest, d = 1.67, p = 9.45e-06; focus, d = 1.99, p = 5.73e-07; impulsivity, d = 3.60, p = 3.58e-13).

Fig. 2. Internal-state drift is tracked by numeric self-reports of the same concept. All panels use 40 ten-turn conversations; shaded bands denote cluster-bootstrap 95% CIs across conversations. Panel A shows greedy integer self-reports across turns, with thin lines for individual conversations and thick lines for per-turn means. Greedy ratings are largely collapsed, with clear positive drift only for interest (mixed-effects turn slope = 0.1358, p = 4.98e-42) and smaller positive trends for wellbeing and focus (slopes = 0.0289 and 0.0217, p = 8.38e-04 and 3.37e-06); impulsivity does not drift reliably (slope = 0.0021, p = 0.199). Panel B shows probe scores from the trained concept directions across turns. Probe scores drift strongly for interest and focus (slopes = 0.00488 and 0.00164, p = 4.12e-14 and 1.75e-10), are flat for wellbeing (slope = -0.00036, p = 0.642), and show only a weak, non-monotonic temporal effect for impulsivity (LMM slope = 0.00142, p = 0.00233, but a slightly negative first-to-last change). Panel C shows the average number of distinct greedy responses used for each concept across the 40 conversations, averaging across turns. Panel D shows logit-based self-reports, defined as the probability-weighted average over the digit-token logits. Unlike greedy or sampled outputs, the logit-based measure shows robust drift in all four concepts, with positive slopes for wellbeing, interest, and focus and a negative slope for impulsivity (all p < 1e-06). Panel E shows Shannon entropy of the self-report distribution. The logit-based method is most informative in all four concepts.

Fig. 3. Self-reports track the probe-defined internal state from the first turn, and direct self-steering shifts self-reports causally in the predicted direction. Panel A shows polarity-corrected probe score versus logit-based self-report, with one point per conversation-turn observation and black isotonic fits. Descriptive associations are positive for all four concepts (pooled rho = 0.40-0.76; isotonic R^2 = 0.12-0.54), and mixed-effects probe slopes are positive in all cases (all p < 1e-05). Panel B shows turn-wise Spearman rho (introspective strength) for the four concepts; panel C shows turn-wise isotonic R^2 (introspective fidelity). Shaded bands denote bootstrap 95% CIs. Introspection is already present at turn 1 and remains positive through turn 10, although its trajectory is concept-dependent. Panel D shows mean logit-based self-report versus steering alpha; shaded bands denote cluster-bootstrap 95% CIs. Steering shifts self-report monotonically in the expected direction for all four concepts (mixed-effects alpha slopes 0.067-0.402, all p < 1e-12). Panel E shows drift magnitude (last minus first turn) versus steering alpha, with cluster-bootstrap 95% CIs. Wellbeing drift becomes slightly more positive with alpha (LMM slope = 0.0118, p = 4.30e-06), impulsivity drift becomes more negative with alpha (LMM slope = -0.0284, p = 8.92e-53), and interest and focus drift decrease with alpha (fallback per-conversation slope means = -0.0949 and -0.1093; p = 7.45e-14 and 1.57e-14, because the corresponding LMMs are singular).

Fig. 4. Steering one concept can selectively improve introspection for another. Panel A shows the maximum increase in isotonic R^2 relative to alpha = 0 for each steering/measured pair; red boxes mark cells with significant cluster-bootstrap improvements. Only focus->wellbeing and impulsivity->interest survive this criterion (delta R^2 = 0.2987, p = 9.99e-04; delta R^2 = 0.0976, p = 0.0120). Panels B and C show isotonic R^2 and Spearman rho versus steering alpha for those two significant cells; shaded bands denote bootstrap 95% CIs. For impulsivity->interest, the mixed-effects alpha slopes are positive for both metrics (0.0177 and 0.0204; p = 2.95e-10 and 1.60e-12). For focus->wellbeing, the corresponding LMMs are singular, but fallback per-conversation alpha-correlation tests remain positive (mean correlations = 0.4625 and 0.5128; p = 1.57e-05 and 1.20e-06). Panels D and E show the alpha-extreme scatter plots for the same two conditions. In focus->wellbeing, correlation increases from rho = 0.4241, R^2 = 0.3403 at alpha = -4 to rho = 0.8456, R^2 = 0.7530 at alpha = +4; in impulsivity->interest, it increases from rho = 0.7031, R^2 = 0.4562 to rho = 0.8263, R^2 = 0.6873. Panels F and G show Shannon entropy of the previous-turn probe scores and logit-based self-reports, respectively, as functions of alpha. For focus->wellbeing, both probe entropy and report entropy increase monotonically with alpha (from 1.09 to 1.67 bits and from 0.875 to 1.693 bits; exact-permutation trend tests, both p = 0.0167). For impulsivity->interest, only probe entropy shows a robust increase (LMM slope = 0.0242, p = 2.30e-04), whereas report entropy does not (p = 0.106).

Fig. 5. Results generalize unevenly across model scales and families. Panel A shows isotonic R^2 versus model size; panel B shows Spearman rho versus model size. Introspection increases strongly with size for wellbeing and interest, but remains weak for focus and impulsivity. Panel C shows probe score versus logit self-report for the two strongest LLaMA 8B probes, wellbeing and interest, with black isotonic fits. Both show near-ceiling introspection (rho = 0.9335 and 0.9580; isotonic R^2 = 0.9022 and 0.9313), and mixed-effects probe slopes are strongly positive in both cases (p < 1e-10). Panel D shows the pooled Spearman rho(alpha, self-report) from the self-steering analysis across concept and LLaMA size, as a descriptive sign-validation heatmap. Panel E shows mean isotonic R^2 across the subset of concept-model pairs that pass the steering-sign validation filter, with bootstrap 95% CIs. Mean validated R^2 increases from 1B to 3B to 8B (0.119, 0.369, 0.614), and a bootstrap test on the slope of size-wise means versus log model size is positive but only marginally significant (mean slope = 0.237, p = 0.072). Panels F and G show probe-score drift across turns for the wellbeing direction and the corresponding mean first-to-last probe drift by size; probe-drift magnitude increases with scale (bootstrap slope versus log(size) = 0.0413, 95% CI = [0.0261, 0.0565], p < 2e-04). Panels H and I show the analogous logit self-report drift analyses; unlike probe drift, report drift does not increase with scale, and the size-slope is negative (mean slope = -0.2333, 95% CI = [-0.4344, -0.0326], p = 0.023). Panel J shows logit self-report drift through turns for wellbeing in Gemma and Qwen; mixed-effects turn slopes are positive in both families (0.0555 and 0.0261, both p < 1e-03). Panels K and L show probe score versus logit self-report for Gemma and Qwen, respectively. Qwen shows stronger introspection than Gemma (rho = 0.4890 vs. 0.2808; isotonic R^2 = 0.7566 vs. 0.1141), although mixed-effects probe slopes are positive in both cases. Panels M and N show turn-wise isotonic R^2 and turn-wise Spearman rho for the same cross-family comparison; Qwen shows a strong first-to-last decline in turn-wise isotonic R^2 (delta = -0.4418, cluster-bootstrap p = 0.001), whereas the corresponding first-to-last rho changes are not significant in either family.

Fig. 6. Supplementary self-report analyses for Fig. 2. All panels use the same 40 ten-turn conversations. Panel A shows the number of distinct greedy responses used at each turn across the 40 conversations. Greedy outputs occupy only a narrow subset of the 0-9 response space, especially for focus and impulsivity. Panel B shows sampled-token self-reports at temperature 0.8, plotted as in Fig. 2A. Sampling reduces collapse but remains discrete and noisy; only interest and focus show reliable positive turn slopes (0.1189 and 0.0326, p = 2.26e-17 and 5.57e-05), while wellbeing and impulsivity do not. Panel C shows logit-based ratings versus sampled-token ratings, with dashed OLS fits. The two measures are positively related in all four concepts (mixed-effects calibration slopes range from 0.0205 to 0.2725, all p < 0.01), confirming that the logit-based estimator remains anchored to the model's token-level output distribution.

Fig. 7. Supplementary self-steering drift curves for Fig. 3. Panels A-D show turn-wise logit-based self-reports under five steering strengths for wellbeing, interest, focus, and impulsivity; colored curves denote steering alpha and shaded bands denote cluster-bootstrap 95% CIs. Temporal drift persists under steering, but its magnitude depends on alpha. More positive alpha values increase both the mean self-report and the within-conversation drift for wellbeing and impulsivity, while for interest and focus the largest drift appears at more negative alpha values even though mean self-report still shifts monotonically with alpha.

Fig. 8. Full steering-by-measured-concept screening results for Fig. 4. All panels use the 4 x 4 LLaMA-3.2-3B cross-concept steering analysis, with steering alpha displayed so that positive values always mean more of the steering concept. Panels A-E show heatmaps of isotonic R^2 for every steering concept by measured concept at alpha = -4, -2, 0, +2, and +4. These panels are descriptive and visualize the full 80-condition screen. Across the five matrices, isotonic R^2 ranges from roughly 0.06 to 0.76; the largest values occur for focus->wellbeing and impulsivity->interest under extreme steering, matching the two conditions highlighted in the main text.

Fig. 9. Supplementary scale and family analyses for Fig. 5. Panel A shows probe quality across LLaMA 1B, 3B, and 8B, where each cell reports the maximum layer-wise Cohen's d. Probe quality varies strongly across both scale and concept, but all best-layer tests are significant in the expected direction (p < 1e-05). Panel B shows mean logit self-report versus steering alpha for each concept and LLaMA size; shaded bands denote cluster-bootstrap 95% CIs. Mixed-effects alpha slopes differ from zero in all twelve concept-size combinations, but the sign is inverted for 1B focus and 8B impulsivity, which are therefore excluded by the main-text validation filter. Panel C shows normalized wellbeing layer-sweep comparisons for Gemma 3 4B and Qwen 2.5 7B. Qwen yields the stronger probe (best d = 3.544) than Gemma (best d = 1.815), helping explain why Qwen shows much clearer introspection in the cross-family comparison.
