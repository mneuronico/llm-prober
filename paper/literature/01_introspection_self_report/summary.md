The study of introspection, self-report, and metacognition in Large Language Models (LLMs) has rapidly evolved into a highly active research area. However, the vast majority of prior work approaches these phenomena through the lenses of factual correctness, confidence calibration, benchmark-driven refinement, or static behavioral self-assessments. Your work distinguishes itself by testing whether **numeric self-reports track probe-defined internal states during natural, multi-turn conversations**, and whether this coupling responds dynamically to interventions over time. 

To contextualize your contribution, the relevant literature can be categorized into four distinct phases of LLM self-assessment research, culminating in the precise gap your paper addresses.

### 1. Foundational Self-Evaluation: Correctness and Confidence
Early investigations into LLM self-awareness focused primarily on whether models "know what they know" regarding factual accuracy and task correctness. For example, Kadavath et al. demonstrated that models can be trained to evaluate the probability that their proposed answers are correct ($P(True)$) or predict if they can answer a question independently ($P(IK)$). Similarly, Yin et al. investigated the ability of LLMs to identify unanswerable questions to prevent hallucinations, while Ren et al. leveraged token-level self-evaluation to improve selective generation by allowing models to abstain from answering when uncertain. These foundational works establish that LLMs possess basic metacognitive monitoring for correctness, but they treat self-assessment strictly as a confidence score rather than an ongoing reflection of internal state.

### 2. Self-Correction and Iterative Refinement
Building on confidence estimation, researchers developed frameworks where models critique and refine their own outputs. Madaan et al. introduced SELF-REFINE, where an LLM generates an initial output, provides natural language feedback to itself, and iteratively improves its response. Shinn et al. proposed Reflexion, which uses verbal reinforcement to help agents learn from prior failings over multiple trials. Recent extensions of this paradigm include Sun and Zeng's "Introspection of Thought" (INoT) for multi-agent logical reasoning within a single LLM, Li and Zhao's dual-loop reflection (extrospection and introspection) for academic response generation, and Zhang et al.'s use of self-evaluation scores as reward signals for factuality alignment. While these studies demonstrate the utility of self-reflection, they are fundamentally benchmark-style task optimizations, focusing on output quality rather than investigating the faithful reporting of internal cognitive states.

### 3. Behavioral Self-Modeling and Psychometric Profiling
More recently, the field has attempted to ask models directly about their own behaviors, preferences, and psychological profiles. Binder et al. showed that models possess a degree of "privileged access," allowing them to predict their own hypothetical behaviors better than other models can. Plunkett et al. expanded on this by fine-tuning LLMs to make decisions based on specific quantitative attribute weights, proving that models could accurately self-report these learned internal decision-making weights in natural language. 

However, evidence for genuine introspection at this behavioral level is highly contested:
*   **Lack of privileged access:** Song, Hu, and Mahowald found that LLMs fail to introspect on their grammatical knowledge, arguing that metalinguistic prompting does not yield privileged access to the model's actual internal probability distributions. 
*   **Misalignment with capability:** Using the psychometric General Self-Efficacy Scale (GSES), Jackson et al. found that LLM self-assessments are highly stable but do not reliably reflect their actual task abilities, suggesting these reports are learned "communication postures" rather than genuine self-assessments.
*   **Temporal inconsistency:** Prestes evaluated "simulated selfhood" and found that while models can mimic introspection, they suffer from "diachronic continuity" failures—meaning they cannot sustain a consistent self-representation over repeated, independent prompts.
*   **Mimicry vs. Causal Introspection:** Comsa and Shanahan argue that true introspection requires a causal connection between an internal state and the self-report (e.g., a model accurately reporting its own temperature parameter), rather than merely mimicking human introspective text. Ackerman also noted that metacognition in LLMs is limited and qualitatively different from humans, relying heavily on external cues rather than internal signals.

### 4. Probing and Controlling Internal Activations
The most advanced related work—and the closest to your methodology—attempts to link self-reports directly to the model's neural activations. Chen et al. utilized linear probing on structural causal games to map internal representations of consciousness concepts (like belief and intention). 

Crucially, recent studies have begun manipulating these states to test awareness:
*   **Concept Injection:** Lindsey evaluated "emergent introspective awareness" by injecting activation patterns (concepts) into a model and asking the model to self-report what it was "thinking". While capable models like Claude Opus 4 could detect these injections, the ability was unreliable and highly context-dependent. Rivera built on this by showing that the ability to detect transient, single-token injected thoughts can be explicitly induced into smaller (7B) models via fine-tuning, turning a rare emergent trait into a reliable capability.
*   **Neurofeedback Paradigms:** Ji-An et al. introduced a neurofeedback approach where LLMs are taught via in-context learning to report and implicitly control specific target axes in their neural activation space. 

### Positioning Your Contribution
Your paper elegantly bridges and advances the gaps left by the literature above. While Plunkett et al. looked at static attribute weights, and Lindsey, Rivera, and Ji-An et al. looked at single-turn reporting of injected activations, **none of these works evaluate continuous, numeric self-reporting of probe-defined internal states over natural, multi-turn interactions.** 

By demonstrating how this coupling behaves from early turns onward, changes over time, and responds to dynamic interventions, your work directly addresses the "diachronic" (temporal) limitations highlighted by Prestes and the "causal connection" requirement demanded by Comsa and Shanahan. You are moving beyond the static "does it know?" benchmark to an operationalized, continuous tracking of an LLM's internal cognitive state through an evolving conversation.
