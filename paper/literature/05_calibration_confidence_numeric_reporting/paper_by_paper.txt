1. Mielke et al. (2022) — Reducing Conversational Agents' Overconfidence
   Through Linguistic Calibration
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Conversational agents are systematically overconfident. Linguistic
  calibration via reinforcement learning can train models to produce hedged
  language that elicits better-calibrated downstream user beliefs.

- Methodology:
  RL-based fine-tuning to optimize linguistic hedging; calibration measured
  as alignment between model's expressed uncertainty and user's downstream
  forecasts; human evaluation studies.

- Relevance to my work:
  Early work on making model-expressed uncertainty more informative.
  Relevant as background for why naïve model outputs overstate certainty —
  paralleling your finding that greedy numeric self-reports collapse to high
  values.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Calibration of hedging language for factual claims, not numeric self-report
  of internal concepts. No probing. No emotive concepts. No multi-turn
  internal state tracking. No comparison with probe-defined state.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as background on the overconfidence problem in LLM reporting, which
  motivates your logit-based approach that bypasses this limitation.

2. Tian et al. (2023) — Just Ask for Calibration: Strategies for Eliciting
   Calibrated Confidence Scores from Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Simply prompting RLHF-tuned LLMs for numeric confidence scores yields
  surprisingly well-calibrated probabilities, competitive with or exceeding
  more complex methods. Verbalized confidence is a viable signal.

- Methodology:
  Prompt LLMs for numeric confidence (0-100%); measure calibration (ECE,
  Brier) against factual accuracy; compare with sampling-based and logit-
  based alternatives.

- Relevance to my work:
  Establishes that prompted numeric confidence is feasible and sometimes
  well-calibrated — the basic operation your self-report extraction builds
  on. However, their calibration is against factual correctness, whereas
  yours is against probe-defined internal state.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Factual correctness calibration only. No emotive concepts. No probing of
  internal state. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No
  comparison of greedy vs. logit-based extraction. No causal steering.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as precedent for numeric confidence elicitation. Contrast by noting
  your target is not correctness calibration but fidelity to internal
  concept state.

3. Manakul et al. (2023) — SelfCheckGPT: Zero-Resource Black-Box Hallucination
   Detection for Generative Large Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  By sampling multiple responses and measuring their informational
  consistency, hallucinations can be detected without ground truth. High
  divergence among samples indicates low confidence and probable
  hallucination.

- Methodology:
  Sample multiple model outputs; compute consistency metrics (n-gram overlap,
  NLI-based contradiction detection, BERTScore); flag divergent outputs as
  hallucinations.

- Relevance to my work:
  Alternative approach to estimating model uncertainty via sampling
  consistency. Your logit-based estimator serves a similar purpose but
  operates at the single-generation level rather than requiring multiple
  samples, making it more efficient for per-turn evaluation.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Hallucination detection, not internal state tracking. No emotive concepts.
  No probing. No numeric self-reports. No multi-turn conversation. No
  temporal dynamics. No comparison with probe-defined state. Requires
  multiple samples per query.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as context for alternative uncertainty estimation methods. Your
  single-forward-pass logit-based approach is more suitable for per-turn
  tracking in conversations.

4. Zhang et al. (2024) — Calibrating the Confidence of Large Language Models
   by Eliciting Fidelity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Models' self-reported confidence can be improved by eliciting fidelity —
  prompting models to assess the trustworthiness of their own reasoning
  process, not just the answer.

- Methodology:
  Multi-step prompting: generate answer → assess reasoning fidelity →
  calibrate confidence; measure calibration against factual accuracy.

- Relevance to my work:
  Another approach to improving the quality of numeric self-reports. However,
  their "fidelity" is about reasoning correctness, not about tracking
  internal concept states.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Factual calibration focus. No emotive concepts. No probing. No multi-
  turn context. No temporal dynamics. No probe-report coupling. No steering.

- Overall takeaway:
  Minor citation. Demonstrates that reasoning about reasoning can improve
  calibration, but in a fundamentally different domain from your emotive
  state tracking.

5. Xie et al. (2024) — Calibrating Reasoning in Language Models with Internal
   Consistency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Inconsistencies between intermediate and final layer representations can
  accurately signal unfaithful chain-of-thought reasoning. Internal
  consistency can serve as a calibration signal.

- Methodology:
  Compare representations at intermediate vs. final layers during reasoning;
  use discrepancies to detect unfaithful reasoning; calibrate confidence
  based on layer agreement.

- Relevance to my work:
  Demonstrates that internal layer representations carry information beyond
  what the final output reveals — paralleling your finding that logit-based
  estimators recover variation invisible in greedy outputs. The idea that
  internal consistency matters connects to your concept coherence analysis.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Reasoning faithfulness, not emotive state tracking. No self-report. No
  multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No emotive concepts. No
  steering.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as support for the idea that internal representations contain
  richer signals than surface outputs, motivating your logit-based
  extraction approach.

6. Wang et al. (2024) — Calibrating Verbalized Probabilities for Large
   Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Verbalized probabilities from LLMs are poorly calibrated but can be
  improved by post-hoc logit scaling, using an "invert softmax" trick to
  approximate underlying logits from verbalized text.

- Methodology:
  Elicit verbalized probabilities; apply temperature scaling and binning
  to correct miscalibration; compare with internal logit-based
  probabilities.

- Relevance to my work:
  Directly relevant to your logit-based self-report methodology. Their
  finding that verbalized probabilities need correction parallels your
  finding that greedy numeric reports collapse and logit-based extraction
  is more informative.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Calibration for factual correctness. No emotive concepts. No probing of
  internal state. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No
  steering. No comparison with independently measured concept directions.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as methodological precedent for the idea that raw verbalized
  numbers are unreliable and logit-level information is needed. Your
  logit-based estimator pursues the same insight in a different domain.

7. Xiong et al. (2024) — Can LLMs Express Their Uncertainty? An Empirical
   Evaluation of Confidence Elicitation in LLMs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs struggle to faithfully express intrinsic uncertainty verbally.
  Numeric self-reports cluster in 80-100% range and appear in multiples
  of five, mimicking human conversational patterns rather than reflecting
  true probabilistic calibration.

- Methodology:
  Systematic evaluation of multiple confidence elicitation methods (verbal,
  numeric, sampling-based); analysis of calibration, discrimination, and
  distribution of expressed confidence across tasks and models.

- Relevance to my work:
  Directly supports your finding that greedy numeric self-reports collapse
  to narrow high-confidence ranges. The clustering in multiples of five
  specifically motivates your logit-based approach that bypasses these
  human-mimicking artifacts.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Uncertainty about factual correctness only. No emotive concepts. No
  probing. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No steering.
  No comparison with internal state measurements.

- Overall takeaway:
  Key citation for motivating your logit-based estimator. Their findings
  about collapsed greedy reports directly parallel yours, and your approach
  provides the solution their work lacks.

8. Kumar et al. (2024) — Confidence Under the Hood: An Investigation into
   the Confidence-Probability Alignment in Large Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  The explicit numeric certainty expressed in LLM responses often misaligns
  with internal token probabilities. There is a fundamental disconnect
  between external verbal reports and internal latent confidence signals.

- Methodology:
  Compare verbalized confidence scores with actual token-level probabilities;
  measure Confidence-Probability Alignment across tasks and models; analyze
  where and why misalignment occurs.

- Relevance to my work:
  Central to your argument. The verbal-internal disconnect is exactly what
  your logit-based estimator addresses. Your probe-report coupling analysis
  extends this beyond probability alignment to concept-state alignment.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Factual confidence focus. No emotive concepts. No probing for concept
  directions. No multi-turn context. No temporal dynamics. No steering.
  Does not propose a method to recover better alignment (only documents
  the problem).

- Overall takeaway:
  Essential citation when introducing your logit-based estimator. Their
  documentation of the verbal-internal disconnect motivates your solution.

9. Farquhar et al. (2024) — Detecting Hallucinations in Large Language Models
   Using Semantic Entropy
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Semantic Entropy — clustering sampled answers by bidirectional entailment
  before computing entropy — provides a highly accurate uncertainty signal
  that measures confidence in meaning rather than specific token sequences,
  solving the surface-form competition problem.

- Methodology:
  Sample multiple outputs; cluster by semantic equivalence (entailment);
  compute entropy over semantic clusters; evaluate on hallucination
  detection and uncertainty benchmarks.

- Relevance to my work:
  Alternative approach to recovering rich uncertainty signals from models.
  Semantic entropy measures meaning-level confidence, while your logit-based
  approach measures token-level probability allocation. Both go beyond
  naive greedy outputs.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Hallucination/uncertainty focus. No emotive concepts. No probing. No
  multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No self-report of internal
  concept state. Requires multiple samples.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as a complementary approach to recovering rich model signals. Your
  single-pass logit-based method is more practical for per-turn tracking.

10. Yona, Aharoni, and Geva (2024) — Can Large Language Models Faithfully
    Express Their Intrinsic Uncertainty in Words?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs frequently fail to faithfully express their intrinsic uncertainty in
  words, generating incorrect information with high verbalized confidence.
  The faithful expression of internal uncertainty remains an unsolved
  problem.

- Methodology:
  Evaluate alignment between model-expressed verbal uncertainty and actual
  internal token-level uncertainty; test across multiple domains and models.

- Relevance to my work:
  Directly motivates your work: if models cannot faithfully express their
  uncertainty verbally, then external measures (probes) and richer
  extraction methods (logit-based) are needed. Your paper provides both.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Uncertainty about factual correctness. No emotive concepts. No probing of
  internal concept directions. No multi-turn context. No temporal dynamics.
  No steering. Does not propose probe-based solutions.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as motivation for why self-reports need independent validation and
  richer extraction methods. Your logit-based estimator plus probe
  comparison addresses exactly the problem they identify.

13. Geng et al. (2024) — A Survey of Confidence Estimation and Calibration
    in Large Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Comprehensive survey establishing that LLM confidence estimation methods
  span verbalized, logit-based, sampling-based, and hybrid approaches, with
  each having distinct strengths and weaknesses. Logit-based methods
  generally offer stronger baseline calibration.

- Methodology:
  Systematic literature review and taxonomy of confidence estimation methods;
  comparison of approaches across dimensions of calibration quality,
  computational cost, and applicability.

- Relevance to my work:
  Situates your logit-based self-report estimator within the broader
  confidence estimation landscape. Their finding that logit-based methods
  outperform verbalized confidence supports your methodological choice.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Survey paper, no new experiments. All methods are evaluated on factual
  correctness, not emotive concept state. No probing. No multi-turn
  setting. No temporal dynamics. No steering.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite for situating your logit-based estimator within the confidence
  estimation literature and for precedent that logit-based methods
  outperform verbalized reports.

14. Bodhwani et al. (2025) — A Calibrated Reflection Approach for Enhancing
    Confidence Estimation in LLMs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Combining Maximum Confidence Selection across labels with a reflection-
  based prompting mechanism forces models to evaluate their initial numeric
  estimates, improving calibration.

- Methodology:
  Calibrated Reflection framework: generate confidence → reflect and
  revise → final calibrated estimate; evaluate on factual QA benchmarks.

- Relevance to my work:
  Another prompting-based approach to improving numeric self-reports.
  The reflection mechanism is tangentially related to the idea that
  self-assessment can be improved, paralleling your finding that steering
  can improve introspection.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Factual calibration. No emotive concepts. No probing. No multi-turn
  conversation. No temporal dynamics. No steering. The improvement
  mechanism is prompting-based, not activation-based.

- Overall takeaway:
  Minor citation for the calibrated numeric self-report literature.

15. Li et al. (2025) — ConfTuner: Training Large Language Models to Express
    Their Confidence Verbally
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Fine-tuning with a tokenized Brier score as a proper scoring rule trains
  models to output numeric probabilities that accurately reflect the
  likelihood of correctness, without requiring proxy labels.

- Methodology:
  Supervised fine-tuning with Brier score loss on numeric confidence tokens;
  evaluate calibration on factual QA benchmarks; compare with baseline
  prompting approaches.

- Relevance to my work:
  Shows that models can be trained to produce better-calibrated numeric
  outputs. Interesting parallel: their approach trains confidence on
  correctness, while your steering approach causally improves self-reports
  of internal concept state.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Requires fine-tuning (not inference-time). Factual correctness target.
  No emotive concepts. No probing. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal
  dynamics. No comparison with independently measured internal state.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite for precedent that numeric self-report quality can be improved via
  training. Contrast with your inference-time steering approach that
  requires no fine-tuning.

16. Zhang et al. (2025) — Direct Confidence Alignment: Aligning Verbalized
    Confidence with Internal Confidence in Large Language Models (DCA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  DPO can directly align a model's generated verbal confidence with its
  internal token-level confidence, treating the latent state as the target
  truth for self-report — bypassing the need for external accuracy labels.

- Methodology:
  Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to align verbalized confidence with
  internal token probabilities; no external factual labels required;
  evaluate calibration improvement.

- Relevance to my work:
  Closest methodological parallel in the calibration literature. DCA aligns
  verbal reports with internal token confidence; your work aligns verbal
  reports with probe-defined concept state. Both treat the internal signal
  as the "ground truth" rather than external accuracy.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Aligns with token-level confidence (surface-level uncertainty), not with
  concept-specific probe directions. Requires training (DPO). No emotive
  concepts. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No activation
  steering. Does not test whether alignment transfers across concepts.

- Overall takeaway:
  Important citation. Your work extends the "align report with internal
  state" idea from token confidence to probe-defined concept directions,
  in a richer experimental setting, without requiring training.

21. Wang and Stengel-Eskin (2026) — Calibrating Verbalized Confidence with
    Self-Generated Distractors (DINCO)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs artificially inflate confidence when a claim is presented in the
  prompt (suggestibility). Normalizing confidence against self-generated
  distractor claims removes this bias and improves calibration.

- Methodology:
  Model generates alternative claims (distractors); confidence in original
  claim is normalized by sum of confidences across all claims; evaluated
  on fact-checking and QA tasks.

- Relevance to my work:
  The suggestibility problem — inflated confidence due to prompt framing —
  is analogous to the concern that your self-report prompts might bias the
  model's numeric response. Your logit-based approach may partially
  mitigate this by extracting implicit probability allocation rather than
  prompted verbalization.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Factual claim verification. No emotive concepts. No probing. No multi-
  turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No steering.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite for the prompt-suggestibility confound, relevant to discussing
  limitations of your self-report prompting methodology.

22. Wang et al. (2026) — Are LLM Decisions Faithful to Verbal Confidence?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Even when LLMs output calibrated verbal confidence scores, they lack the
  strategic agency to act on them. When facing high-penalty scenarios for
  incorrect answers, models fail to adjust abstention rates accordingly.
  Verbalized confidence does not reliably govern downstream behavior.

- Methodology:
  Test whether LLMs adjust behavioral strategies (abstention, answer
  revision) based on their own stated confidence; manipulate penalty
  structures; compare stated confidence with behavioral adaptation.

- Relevance to my work:
  Reveals a dissociation between expressed confidence and behavioral
  control. Analogous to the broader question of whether self-reports
  actually reflect functional internal states. Your probe-based framework
  tests this alignment directly.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Behavioral decision-making, not internal state tracking. No emotive
  concepts. No probing. No multi-turn context. No temporal dynamics.
  No steering. No concept direction comparison.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as evidence that self-reports and behavior can dissociate, further
  motivating the need for independent internal state measurement (probes)
  to validate what models report.
