The evaluation of confidence, uncertainty estimation, and calibration in Large Language Models (LLMs) represents a critical frontier in ensuring their reliable deployment in high-stakes domains. While traditional machine learning calibration focuses on aligning predicted probabilities with ground-truth accuracy, LLMs introduce unique complexities due to their open-ended generation capabilities, the semantic equivalence of varied outputs, and their ability to directly converse about their own uncertainty. The surrounding literature provides strong methodological precedent for eliciting confidence, recovering rich uncertainty estimates from latent signals, and identifying the gaps between a model's internal state and its external reporting.

### Precedent for Eliciting Confidence Numerically and Verbally
A fundamental approach to understanding LLM uncertainty is prompting models to explicitly state their confidence, either as linguistic hedges (e.g., "I'm not sure, but...") or as numeric probabilities (e.g., "80% confidence"). This "verbalized confidence" leverages the natural language capabilities of LLMs to provide interpretable uncertainty estimates without requiring access to internal model weights or logits. 

However, empirical evaluations reveal that LLMs struggle to faithfully express their intrinsic uncertainty. Models frequently exhibit severe overconfidence, generating incorrect information accompanied by high verbalized confidence. Furthermore, their numeric self-reports often mimic human conversational patterns, clustering heavily in the 80%–100% range and frequently appearing in multiples of five, rather than reflecting true probabilistic calibration. 

To improve the quality of numeric and verbal reporting, several tuning and prompting strategies have been proposed:
*   **Supervised Fine-Tuning and RLHF:** Methods like **ConfTuner** fine-tune models using a tokenized Brier score as a proper scoring rule, training the model to output numeric probabilities that accurately reflect the likelihood of correctness without relying on proxy labels. Similarly, multitask fine-tuning on metacognitive tasks (like single-question confidence estimation and pairwise confidence comparison) has been shown to improve both calibration and discrimination across disparate domains. Linguistic calibration has also been optimized via reinforcement learning to ensure long-form generations elicit calibrated downstream forecasts from users.
*   **Prompting and Self-Reflection:** Frameworks like **Calibrated Reflection** combine Maximum Confidence Selection across all possible labels with a reflection-based prompting mechanism to force models to evaluate their initial numeric estimates. 

Despite these improvements in stating confidence, a critical dissociation remains. Research indicates that even when LLMs output calibrated verbal confidence scores, they lack the strategic agency to act on them. When exposed to scenarios with massive penalties for incorrect answers, LLMs generally fail to adjust their abstention rates, revealing that verbalized confidence does not reliably govern their downstream decision-making.

### Recovering Richer Uncertainty Estimates 
Because naive greedy outputs and single-generation likelihoods often fail to capture true epistemic uncertainty, researchers have developed consistency-based and semantic metrics to recover richer signals from the model.

*   **Consistency and Sampling:** By sampling multiple responses at a high temperature, methods like **SelfCheckGPT** calculate informational consistency; if the sampled responses diverge or contradict each other, it indicates high uncertainty and a likelihood of hallucination.
*   **Semantic Entropy:** Farquhar et al. introduce **Semantic Entropy**, which clusters sampled answers based on bidirectional entailment before computing entropy. This addresses the issue of surface-form competition, recovering a highly accurate uncertainty signal that measures confidence in the *meaning* of the text rather than the specific token sequence. Lamb et al. further establish that applying simple scalar temperature scaling to token generation drastically improves the calibration of these semantic-level confidence distributions.
*   **Distractor Normalization:** To combat "suggestibility"—where an LLM artificially inflates its numeric confidence in a claim simply because it was presented in the prompt—Wang and Stengel-Eskin propose **DINCO**. This method normalizes verbalized confidence against the sum of confidences assigned to self-generated distractors (alternative claims), bridging the gap between validation coherence and generation consistency.

### Comparing Expressed Confidence with Latent Model Signals
A central theme in recent literature is the fundamental disconnect between a model's external verbal reports and its internal latent state. Studies mapping **Confidence-Probability Alignment** reveal that the explicit numeric certainty expressed in an LLM's response often misaligns with its internal confidence, as quantified by the actual token probabilities.

To address this, researchers have begun recovering and directly evaluating latent signals:
*   **Logit-Based Estimators:** While black-box numeric reporting is easy to elicit, logit-based probabilities generally offer stronger baseline calibration. To bridge the gap, methods have been developed to apply post-hoc logit scaling (like temperature scaling or QA-binning) directly to verbalized probabilities by using an "invert softmax" trick to approximate underlying logits from the verbalized text.
*   **Latent State Probing:** Models internally encode correctness and reasoning convergence within their hidden representations. Wu et al. demonstrate **Thought Calibration**, using lightweight linear probes on a model's hidden layers to dynamically predict reasoning consistency and terminate generation. Similarly, Xie et al. uncover that inconsistencies between intermediate and final layer representations can accurately signal unfaithful chain-of-thought reasoning.
*   **Direct Confidence Alignment (DCA):** Because ground-truth factual calibration is difficult for models to self-assess, Zhang et al. bypass accuracy altogether. They use Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to directly align the model's generated verbal confidence with its internal token confidence, treating the latent state as the target truth for the model's self-report.

### Methodological Bridge: Internal Concept States vs. Factual Correctness
The literature provides extensive precedent for extracting both verbal self-reports and latent logit/probe-based signals to estimate uncertainty. However, the vast majority of these methods are strictly oriented around *factual correctness*—attempting to calibrate a model so that its numeric confidence matches the objective probability that an answer is right.
