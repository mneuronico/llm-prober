**The necessity of evaluating Large Language Models (LLMs) dynamically across multi-turn interactions is driven by the severe temporal degradation of their internal states, personas, and attention mechanisms.** Recent literature consistently demonstrates that single-shot or pooled evaluations fail to capture how a model’s behavior and introspection drift as a conversation progresses. To accurately assess an LLM's internal state, introspection must be measured on a turn-by-turn basis, carefully separating genuine state updates from artifacts caused merely by the accumulation of conversational turns.

Here is a synthesis of the evidence supporting the need for per-turn analysis, categorized by the types of state drift observed in recent research.

### **Persona and Identity Drift Across Turns**
When LLMs are tasked with maintaining a specific identity or persona, their consistency demonstrably degrades over multi-turn interactions. 
*   **Vulnerability to Fictitious Hallucinations:** Research simulating 36-turn conversations reveals that **LLMs suffer from significant "identity drift," with larger models actually experiencing greater drift than smaller ones**. As the conversation length increases, larger models tend to introduce new, fictitious details about themselves, accepting this newly fabricated information as a credible source of their identity and causing their responses to fluctuate.
*   **Contradiction Insensitivity:** Even the best-performing persona-based generative models are highly insensitive to contradictory words, routinely failing to deliver consistent personas over time. Without explicit, step-by-step consistency constraints, models act more like plain language models than persona-conditioned agents, outputting contradictory statements.
*   **Multi-Dimensional Consistency Failures:** When acting as simulated human users, off-the-shelf LLMs frequently drift from their assigned roles, suddenly changing stylistic tone or instantly "curing" simulated mental health conditions. Evaluations using prompt-to-line, line-to-line, and Q&A consistency metrics prove that **surface-level coherence across turns often masks deep underlying inconsistencies in the model's supposedly stable beliefs**. 

### **Instruction Instability and Attention Decay**
The mechanical root of conversation-induced state drift can be traced directly to how transformers allocate attention over time. 
*   **Instruction Drift and Persona Hijacking:** Over the course of extended dialogue (e.g., 8 rounds), models exhibit severe "instruction drift". Not only do agents gradually stop following their initial system prompts, but they are also **highly susceptible to adopting the instructions or persona of the user they are interacting with**. 
*   **Mechanical Attention Decay:** This drift is caused by the phenomenon of *attention decay*. While generating responses, the percentage of attention the model allocates to the initial system prompt drops sharply across successive conversational turns. Because the mathematical space of possible outputs enlarges over time as user inputs are added, the influence of the initial prompt inherently wanes. 
*   **Serial Position Effects (SPE):** The position of information within the context window alters how it is processed. LLMs suffer heavily from primacy and recency biases, meaning their attention shifts dramatically depending on where information is located in the sequence. As prompt and conversation length increases, the model's focus dynamically shifts, further proving that an LLM's internal state is highly dependent on the current temporal position of the interaction.

### **Confidence Calibration and the Confound of Conversation Time**
To properly measure a model's introspection (such as confidence or certainty), evaluations cannot treat confidence as a static attribute of a single response. In multi-turn settings, information arrives incrementally, and a model's confidence should theoretically increase monotonically as ambiguity is resolved.
*   **The Turn-Count Confound:** Crucially, **models often artificially increase their self-reported confidence simply because the conversation has gotten longer, not because they have gained genuine task-relevant information**. When tested with "placebo hints" (turns that add conversational history without adding useful clues), many standard confidence estimation methods (like asking the model if its answer is "True") mistakenly increase their confidence scores. 
*   **The Need for Per-Turn Probing:** Because standard verbalized confidence is easily confounded by turn count, researchers must evaluate models turn-by-turn to disentangle genuine information gain from conversational filler. Probes specifically designed to ask if the current information is *sufficient* (e.g., `P(SUFFICIENT)`) are required to actively penalize uninformative turns and track actual evidence accumulation rather than just reacting to the length of the context window.
*   **Interaction vs. Summarization:** The dynamic structure of the conversation itself alters the model's introspective state. While models may achieve similar accuracy when reading a single-turn summary versus participating in a multi-turn interaction, their *confidence signals* behave entirely differently between the two formats. This proves that **the interactive, temporal structure of the dialogue actively manipulates the model's internal confidence state.**

### **Conclusion for Your Paper**
These findings collectively validate your argument: **Introspection cannot be reliably measured in a pooled or single-shot setting because the LLM's internal state—dictated by attention allocation, persona adherence, and confidence calibration—is in a constant state of flux.** To establish a rigorous methodology, experiments must measure state variables on a turn-by-turn basis. Furthermore, as demonstrated by the "placebo hint" experiments, researchers must actively isolate genuine probe-report coupling (actual internal state updates) from spurious correlations driven merely by the inflation of the context window over conversation time.
