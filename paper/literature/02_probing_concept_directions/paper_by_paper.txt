PAPER-BY-PAPER ANALYSIS — 02 Probing Internal Representations and Concept Directions

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. Kim et al. (2018) — Interpretability Beyond Feature Attribution: Quantitative
   Testing with Concept Activation Vectors (TCAV)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  High-level human-understandable concepts can be represented as linear
  directions (Concept Activation Vectors, CAVs) in neural network activation
  spaces. Directional derivatives along CAVs quantify how sensitive a model's
  predictions are to each concept.

- Methodology:
  Train linear classifiers to separate concept examples from random examples
  in activation space; the normal to the decision boundary is the CAV.
  Compute directional derivatives of model outputs along CAVs to test causal
  relevance. Statistical testing with random concept baselines.

- Relevance to my work:
  Foundational for the concept direction methodology. My probe-defined
  internal state directions are conceptually descended from CAVs. TCAV's
  directional derivative idea also relates to my causal steering tests.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Designed for image classification CNNs, not language models. No multi-turn
  conversation. No self-report or introspection. No temporal dynamics. No
  emotive concepts. Not applied to LLMs or text generation.

- Overall takeaway:
  Essential foundational citation for concept direction methodology. Frame
  your probes as inheriting from this tradition while extending it to LLM
  internal state tracking and introspection measurement.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. Alain and Bengio (2018) — Understanding Intermediate Layers Using Linear
   Classifier Probes
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Linear classifier probes applied to intermediate layers of deep networks
  reveal that representations become progressively more linearly separable
  (for the task objective) at deeper layers. Probes serve as "thermometers"
  for measuring information at different network locations.

- Methodology:
  Train linear classifiers on frozen intermediate representations at each
  layer; track classification accuracy across layers; compare with the
  network's overall performance.

- Relevance to my work:
  Provides the original "probe as thermometer" metaphor that directly applies
  to your use of probes as operational measurement tools. Justifies using
  linear probes over complex non-linear ones.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Applied to standard classification networks, not LLMs. No concept-specific
  probing (just task labels). No multi-turn conversational context. No
  self-report comparison. No temporal dynamics. No emotive concepts.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as the foundational reference for linear probing methodology and the
  "probes as measurement instruments" framing that your work adopts.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. Hewitt and Manning (2019) — A Structural Probe for Finding Syntax in Word
   Representations
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Linear transformations of contextual word representations encode syntactic
  tree structure: squared L2 distances between transformed vectors correlate
  with parse tree distances between words.

- Methodology:
  Train a linear transformation (structural probe) such that the distance
  between transformed word embeddings mirrors syntactic tree distance.
  Evaluate on dependency parsing tasks.

- Relevance to my work:
  Demonstrates that linear probes can capture rich, structured information
  (not just binary labels) from representations. Supports the idea that
  concept directions encode meaningful geometry.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Focused on syntactic structure, not emotive or affective concepts. No
  self-report. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal tracking. No causal
  interventions. No LLM-scale models.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as evidence that linear probes can capture structured geometric
  information from representations, supporting the validity of your linear
  probes for concept directions.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. Hewitt and Liang (2019) — Designing and Interpreting Probes with Control
   Tasks
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  High probe accuracy alone does not prove that a representation "contains"
  a concept — complex probes can memorize the task from word types alone.
  Probe "selectivity" (accuracy on real task minus accuracy on random control
  task) is the key metric.

- Methodology:
  Define control tasks where word types are mapped to random outputs. Measure
  probe accuracy on both real tasks and control tasks. Selectivity =
  real accuracy - control accuracy. Argue that good probes must have high
  selectivity.

- Relevance to my work:
  Directly motivates the need for probe validation and controls. Supports
  your use of random-vector controls and other validation methods to ensure
  your probes are not learning surface artifacts.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Focused on syntactic probing in NLP models. No emotive concepts. No
  multi-turn conversational setting. No self-report comparison. No temporal
  dynamics. No causal steering.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite when justifying your probe validation methodology (random-vector
  controls, held-out contrastive separation). Your work directly addresses
  the selectivity concern they raise.

5. Pimentel et al. (2020) — Information-Theoretic Probing for Linguistic
   Structure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Probing should be understood as estimating the mutual information between a
  representation and a target property. Contextual embeddings cannot contain
  more information than the raw input, so probes measure "ease of extraction"
  (how accessibly structured the information is) rather than raw presence.

- Methodology:
  Information-theoretic framework for probing; minimum description length
  analysis; comparison of probe complexity with extraction accuracy.

- Relevance to my work:
  Provides the theoretical foundation for why linear probes are preferred:
  they measure whether information is accessibly structured, not just present.
  Your probes capture how easily the model's own representations can be read
  for concept information.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Purely methodological and linguistic. No emotive concepts. No LLMs. No
  multi-turn conversation. No self-report. No temporal dynamics. No causal
  tests.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite for the theoretical justification that probes measure accessible
  structure, not just information presence. This supports your framing of
  probes as operational tools.

6. Burns et al. (2022) — Discovering Latent Knowledge in Language Models
   Without Supervision (CCS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Contrast-Consistent Search (CCS) can discover latent knowledge (e.g.,
  truth vs. falsehood directions) in LLM activation space without any
  supervised labels, by enforcing logical consistency constraints (a
  statement and its negation must have opposite truth values).

- Methodology:
  Unsupervised identification of concept directions using consistency loss;
  contrastive pairs of statements and negations; evaluated on truth/falsehood
  detection benchmarks.

- Relevance to my work:
  Your contrastive concept training methodology is conceptually related — you
  also train directions from contrastive pairs. CCS shows that these
  directions can be found even without supervision, validating the linear
  representation hypothesis.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Focused on truth/falsehood concepts. No emotive or affective concepts. No
  self-report. No multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No causal
  steering. Single-shot classification setting.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as evidence supporting the linear representation hypothesis and
  contrastive methods for identifying concept directions. Your work extends
  this to emotive concepts and links the discovered directions to self-report.

7. Belinkov (2022) — Probing Classifiers: Promises, Shortcomings, and Advances
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Comprehensive review establishing that probing classifiers are useful but
  inherently correlational. High probe accuracy does not guarantee that the
  model causally uses the probed information. Probes must be validated
  through convergent evidence.

- Methodology:
  Survey and critical analysis of the probing literature; taxonomization of
  probe types, validation methods, and failure modes.

- Relevance to my work:
  Directly supports your framing of probes as operational tools requiring
  convergent validation. Your use of steering as causal validation and
  self-report as behavioral evidence addresses exactly the concerns raised
  here.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Survey paper, no new experiments. Does not propose solutions for the
  correlation-causation gap beyond flagging the problem. No emotive concepts.
  No multi-turn setting. No self-report framework.

- Overall takeaway:
  Essential methodological citation. Cite to justify why your probes need
  validation and why your steering tests provide the causal evidence that
  probing alone cannot deliver.

8. Gurnee and Tegmark (2023) — Language Models Represent Space and Time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs construct linear, multi-scale spatial and temporal maps in their
  hidden representations. Non-linear probes offer minimal improvement over
  linear ones, strongly supporting linear representation of continuous
  concepts.

- Methodology:
  Linear ridge regression and MLP probes trained on geographic coordinates
  and temporal dates from LLM hidden layers; comparison of linear vs.
  non-linear probe performance; neuron-level analysis.

- Relevance to my work:
  Strong precedent for linear representation of abstract, continuous concepts
  in LLMs. Supports your assumption that emotive concepts can also be
  linearly represented. Also demonstrates layer-wise analysis of where
  concepts are represented.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Spatial and temporal concepts, not emotive states. No self-report. No
  multi-turn conversational setting. No temporal dynamics of concept
  evolution within conversations. No causal steering tests on behavior.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as strong evidence for the linear representation hypothesis applied
  to continuous abstract concepts, which your work extends to emotive states.

9. Azaria and Mitchell (2023) — The Internal State of an LLM Knows When It's
   Lying
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs maintain an internal representation that distinguishes truthful from
  false statements, even when the model's output text is a hallucination.
  Classifiers on hidden states can predict truthfulness with high accuracy.

- Methodology:
  Train classifiers on hidden layer activations to detect truthfulness;
  compare internal-state-based predictions with output-based methods;
  evaluate across multiple datasets and model architectures.

- Relevance to my work:
  Demonstrates that internal representations contain information the model
  does not always express in its outputs — directly paralleling your finding
  that greedy self-reports collapse while internal state continues to vary.
  Also supports the probe methodology for detecting internal states.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Truthfulness is the concept, not emotive states. No self-report analysis.
  No multi-turn conversation. No temporal dynamics. No steering. No
  comparison of probe-defined state with model's own reporting.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as evidence that internal states can diverge from outputs, and that
  probes can detect what outputs miss. This directly motivates your logit-
  based estimator that recovers variation invisible in greedy reports.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. McKenzie et al. (2025) — Detecting High-Stakes Interactions with
    Activation Probes
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  Activation probes can serve as efficient monitors to detect "high-stakes"
  or potentially harmful user interactions, matching the performance of
  much larger fine-tuned models at a fraction of the computational cost.

- Methodology:
  Train lightweight linear probes on intermediate activations to classify
  interactions as high-stakes vs. benign; compare with full model fine-tuning
  and larger model baselines.

- Relevance to my work:
  Practical demonstration that lightweight probes on internal representations
  can serve as real-time monitors — similar to your per-turn probe-based
  measurement of internal state during conversations.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Binary classification of interaction type, not continuous tracking of
  emotive state. No self-report. No multi-turn temporal analysis. No
  steering. No comparison with model's own numeric reports.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as a practical application demonstrating the utility of activation
  probes for monitoring, paralleling your use of probes as continuous state
  monitors during conversation.
