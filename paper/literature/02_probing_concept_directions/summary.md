**Probing Internal Representations and Behavioral Concept Directions: A Comprehensive Report**

### 1. Justifying the Use of Linear Probes and Concept Directions
Probes serve as critical operational tools for illuminating the opaque internal states of deep neural networks. Conceived as "thermometers" used to measure information simultaneously across different locations in a network, probes are independently trained classifiers that map intermediate model representations to specific properties without altering the underlying model.

The use of **Concept Activation Vectors (CAVs)** and linear concept directions provides a robust mathematical foundation for this measurement apparatus. By defining a high-level concept using a set of examples, we can train a linear classifier to separate these examples from random counterexamples in the model's activation space; the vector orthogonal to this decision boundary becomes the linear direction representing the concept. 

There is strong empirical support for the hypothesis that language models represent complex, real-world concepts linearly. Recent studies demonstrate that highly abstract properties can be accurately decoded using simple linear transformations:
*   **Space and Time:** LLMs construct linear, multi-scale spatial and temporal maps (e.g., coordinates for cities or publication dates of historical events) across their hidden layers. Comparing linear ridge regression probes with highly expressive non-linear multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) reveals that non-linear probes offer minimal performance improvements, strongly supporting the hypothesis that these continuous features are represented linearly.
*   **Truthfulness and Deception:** LLMs maintain an internal state that distinguishes truthful statements from false ones, even when the model's outward generation produces a hallucination. Classifiers trained on hidden layer activations can predict the truthfulness of a statement with high accuracy, outperforming methods that rely merely on the output probabilities of the text. 
*   **High-Stakes Interactions:** Activation probes can function as highly efficient, reliable monitors to detect "high-stakes" or potentially harmful user interactions, matching the performance of much larger finetuned LLMs at a fraction of the computational cost.

Furthermore, **linear probes are broadly preferred over complex non-linear probes because they serve as a better measure of the model's inherent representations**. Highly expressive probes run the risk of memorizing surface-level patterns and independently learning the task, rather than reflecting the structure actually embedded in the model. Linear models enforce a strict bottleneck, ensuring that if a concept is successfully decoded, it must be explicitly and accessibly represented by the LLM. 

### 2. The Shortcomings of Probes: Memorization vs. Representation
While probe scores are useful, **high probing accuracy alone does not settle what a model "really" represents.** The probing paradigm faces several inherent theoretical and methodological limitations:

*   **The Selectivity Problem:** A highly accurate probe might simply have the capacity to learn a task from word types rather than extracting linguistic structure from the contextual embeddings. When evaluated against "control tasks" (where word types are assigned random outputs), complex probes often achieve high accuracy but low *selectivity* (the difference between real task accuracy and control task accuracy). A good probe must be highly selective, proving it is evaluating the model's contextual representations rather than just memorizing vocabulary.
*   **Information-Theoretic Tensions:** From an information-theoretic perspective, probing estimates the mutual information between a representation and a property. Under this strict interpretation, contextual embeddings cannot contain *more* information than the raw input sentence itself. Therefore, the utility of a probe relies on measuring the **"ease of extraction"**—demonstrating that the model has structured the information in a way that is easily accessible to linear downstream consumers, rather than proving the raw presence of information.
*   **Correlation vs. Causation:** The most significant limitation of the probing paradigm is that a probe only identifies *correlations* between hidden states and external properties. If a probe successfully extracts a concept from a layer, it guarantees that the information is encoded there, but it does not guarantee that the original model actually *uses* this information to make its predictions. 

### 3. The Need for Convergent Evidence: Structure, Validation, and Steering
Because probing classifiers are correlational operational tools, their results must be validated through convergent evidence to prove that a discovered direction is scientifically meaningful and causally relevant to the model's behavior. 

**Structural Validations**
One way to validate concept directions is to test whether the geometry of the vector space maps to the structural geometry of the concept. For instance, "structural probes" do not just classify properties; they identify linear transformations where the squared L2 distance between word vectors perfectly correlates with the path distance between words in a syntactic parse tree. Similarly, unsupervised methods like Contrast-Consistent Search (CCS) validate that a model's internal representation of "truth" satisfies logical constraints (e.g., a statement and its negation must have opposite probabilities), successfully discovering latent knowledge without any supervised labels.

**Steering and Causal Interventions**
To bridge the gap between correlation and causation, probing must be paired with interventional techniques (steering). **If a concept direction is genuinely used by the model, intervening along that vector should predictably alter the model's downstream behavior.**
*   **Directional Derivatives:** Methods like TCAV compute the directional derivative of a model's output prediction along the concept vector. If modifying the internal state toward the concept direction changes the output class probability, it proves the model relies on that concept for its decision.
*   **Ablations and Additions:** Causal interventions—such as zero-ablating specific neurons associated with a probed feature, or adding vectors back into the representation—provide definitive proof of utility. For instance, when researchers zero-ablated "time neurons" or pinned their activations to specific values, the model's output predictably shifted the generated dates of historical events.
*   **Counterfactual Probing:** By utilizing gradients from a probe to erase or modify specific properties in the representation space (creating amnesic or counterfactual representations), researchers can observe the degradation in the model's primary task performance. If removing the probed concept alters the model's natural output, it confirms the causal role of that feature.

**Conclusion for Your Section Framing:**
The literature establishes that linear probes and concept directions are highly effective, computationally efficient mechanisms for identifying latent concepts like truthfulness, spatial awareness, and risk. However, because highly expressive probes can hallucinate structure through memorization, and because extraction does not guarantee causal usage, probes must be treated strictly as operational tools. To conclusively determine what a model "really" represents, probe measurements must be triangulated with structural consistency checks and causal steering interventions, providing the convergent evidence necessary to validate behavioral concept directions.
