1. Coda-Forno et al. (2022) — Language Models Can Generate Human-Like
   Self-Reports of Emotion
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs can produce self-reports of emotional states that resemble human
  emotional self-reports in structure and pattern, suggesting that the
  statistical regularities of emotional language are well-captured by
  language models.

- Methodology:
  Prompt LLMs to self-report emotional states in response to scenarios;
  compare structure and patterns of LLM emotional self-reports with human
  baselines; analyze dimensional structure of reported emotions.

- Relevance to my work:
  Establishes that LLMs can produce emotionally structured self-reports,
  which is a prerequisite for your work. If models could not produce
  meaningful emotional self-reports at all, your probe-report coupling
  analysis would have no foundation.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Tests whether reports "look human-like" (surface similarity), not whether
  they track actual internal states. No probing of internal representations.
  No independent measurement of internal emotive state. No multi-turn
  conversational setting. No temporal dynamics. No causal steering. No
  logit-based estimation. No probe-report coupling analysis.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as early evidence that LLMs can produce structured emotional
  self-reports. Your work asks the much deeper question: do those reports
  actually track an independently measured internal state?

2. Coda-Forno, Witte, Jagadish, Binz, Akata, and Schulz (2023) — Inducing
   Anxiety in Large Language Models Can Induce Bias
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs respond robustly to anxiety questionnaires, producing comparable
  anxiety scores to humans. Anxiety-inducing prompts predictably increase
  both anxiety questionnaire scores and behavioral biases (racism, ageism).
  Greater anxiety-inducing text leads to stronger bias increases.

- Methodology:
  Administer standardized psychiatric anxiety questionnaires (GAD-7/similar)
  to 12 LLMs; induce anxiety via prompt manipulation; measure effect on
  anxiety scores and on established bias benchmarks; dose-response analysis
  linking anxiety intensity to bias magnitude.

- Relevance to my work:
  Highly relevant. Demonstrates that emotive states (anxiety) can be
  induced in LLMs and that these states causally affect downstream behavior,
  establishing that emotive concepts are not merely surface text but have
  functional consequences. The dose-response finding (more anxiety → more
  bias) parallels your finding that steering strength modulates self-report.
  Also relevant: they use a concept (anxiety) that you also probe.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Induces emotion via prompt manipulation, not via activation steering.
  Measures behavioral outcomes (bias benchmarks), not internal
  representation tracking with probes. No comparison of self-report with
  independently measured internal state. No multi-turn conversational
  dynamics. No temporal evolution analysis. No logit-based self-report
  extraction. No probe-report coupling measurement. Single-shot
  questionnaire format.

- Overall takeaway:
  Key citation for the functional reality of emotive states in LLMs. The
  dose-response anxiety→bias link establishes that emotive concepts have
  causal behavioral consequences, supporting your claim that probing and
  steering emotive directions is scientifically meaningful. Your work adds
  the internal measurement dimension (probes) and the temporal tracking
  that this paper lacks.

3. Huang et al. (2023) — Emotionally Numb or Empathetic? Evaluating How LLMs
   Feel Using EmotionBench
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs can generally respond appropriately to emotion-eliciting situations,
  but they fall short in alignment with human emotional behavior patterns
  and cannot establish connections between similar situations. Uses emotion
  appraisal theory from psychology to evaluate eight emotions across 36
  situational factors.

- Methodology:
  Collect 400+ emotion-eliciting situations validated by 1,200+ human
  subjects worldwide; administer to 7 LLMs (GPT-4, Mixtral, LLaMA-3.1,
  etc.); compare LLM emotional responses with human baselines; categorize
  into 36 factors across 8 emotions. Published at NeurIPS 2024.

- Relevance to my work:
  Provides a systematic evaluation of LLM emotional responses grounded in
  psychological appraisal theory. Establishes that LLMs have partial but
  imperfect emotional response capabilities, motivating the need for your
  deeper investigation of whether these responses connect to internal
  state.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Evaluates behavioral responses to situations (output text), not internal
  representation tracking. No probing of internal concept directions. No
  comparison of self-report with probe-defined state. No multi-turn
  conversation. No temporal dynamics. No activation steering. No
  logit-based estimation. Single-shot scenario evaluation. No causal
  testing of whether emotional responses depend on internal emotive state.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as a comprehensive benchmark for LLM emotional responses. Note that
  EmotionBench evaluates whether models produce appropriate emotional
  outputs, while your work tests whether models can accurately report
  independently measured internal emotive states — a fundamentally deeper
  question.

4. Sabour et al. (2024) — EmoBench: Evaluating the Emotional Intelligence
   of Large Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs show a considerable gap compared to average human emotional
  intelligence. Proposes a comprehensive definition of machine EI including
  Emotional Understanding and Emotional Application, with 400 hand-crafted
  questions requiring thorough reasoning. Published at ACL 2024.

- Methodology:
  Design benchmark based on psychological EI theories; create 400 hand-
  crafted questions in English and Chinese; require emotional understanding
  (recognizing, predicting emotions) and emotional application (using
  emotions to facilitate thought, regulate emotions); evaluate multiple LLMs
  vs. human baselines.

- Relevance to my work:
  Establishes that emotional intelligence in LLMs is limited, motivating
  the need for better tools to measure and understand emotive processing.
  The distinction between emotional understanding and application connects
  to your decomposition between internal state (understanding) and
  self-report (application/expression).

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Benchmark evaluation of emotional reasoning ability, not tracking of
  internal emotive states. No probing of internal representations. No
  self-report of internal concept state. No multi-turn conversation. No
  temporal dynamics. No activation steering. No logit-based estimation.
  No probe-report coupling. Tests task performance, not introspection.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite as background establishing that LLM emotional capability is limited
  and that the field needs better methods for evaluating emotive processing
  — which your probe-based framework provides at the representation level.

5. Fazzi, Hinge, Heinrich, and Burelli (2025) — Don't Get Too Excited:
   Eliciting Emotions in LLMs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  State-of-the-art LLMs show significant variation in their ability to
  maintain consistent affect (arousal and valence) during extended multi-
  turn dialogues. Some models maintain stable emotional trajectories while
  others fluctuate. Models struggle to produce and maintain extreme
  emotional states and to adapt affect to changing conversational contexts.

- Methodology:
  Multi-turn dialogue simulations between LLMs; quantify affective
  expressive range using Russell's circumplex model (arousal and valence
  axes); LLM-based sentiment analysis to evaluate emotional expression;
  measure trajectory stability across conversation turns.

- Relevance to my work:
  Very closely related: also studies emotive expression across multi-turn
  conversations and uses the arousal-valence dimensional framework. Their
  finding that emotional trajectories fluctuate across turns directly
  parallels your observation that probe-defined internal state drifts
  through conversation. The multi-turn dialogue paradigm is similar to
  your experimental setup.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Measures emotional expression in output text (via external sentiment
  analysis), not internal representation tracking with probes. No
  comparison of expressed emotion with independently measured internal
  emotive state. No probe-defined concept directions. No self-report of
  internal state (just expressed emotion in text). No activation steering.
  No logit-based estimation. No probe-report coupling analysis. No causal
  testing. Uses external LLM-based sentiment analysis rather than internal
  probes for measurement.

- Overall takeaway:
  Important citation as the most methodologically similar prior work in the
  emotive states space. Both your work and theirs study emotive dynamics
  across multi-turn conversations, but they measure the output side
  (expressed emotion in text) while you measure the internal side (probe-
  defined activation state) AND the output side (numeric self-report),
  and then test their coupling. Your work adds the internal measurement
  dimension, causal steering, and probe-report coupling analysis.

6. Ishikawa and Yoshino (2025) — AI with Emotions: Exploring Emotional
   Expressions in Large Language Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs (GPT, Gemini, Llama3, Cohere) can successfully role-play as agents
  with specified emotional states using Russell's circumplex model (arousal
  and valence axes). Sentiment analysis confirms that generated responses
  are consistent with the specified emotional states, demonstrating LLM
  capability for emotional expression.

- Methodology:
  Instruct LLMs to role-play with specified emotional states defined on
  Russell's circumplex model; evaluate generated responses using a
  sentiment analysis model trained on GoEmotions; measure consistency
  between specified and expressed emotions. Published at NLP4DH workshop,
  NAACL 2025.

- Relevance to my work:
  Shows that LLMs can produce outputs consistent with instructed emotional
  states, which is conceptually similar to your steering experiments
  (shifting internal state and observing output changes). The circumplex
  model framing (continuous arousal-valence space) connects to your
  treatment of emotive concepts as continuous dimensions.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Emotional expression via role-play instruction, not via internal
  activation steering. No probing of internal representations. No
  comparison with independently measured internal state. No naturalistic
  conversation (explicitly instructed to feel specific emotions). No
  temporal dynamics of emotion emerging naturally. No self-report of
  internal state (just role-played output). No logit-based estimation.
  No probe-report coupling. No causal testing beyond instruction-following.

- Overall takeaway:
  Cite for the demonstration that LLMs can express emotions along
  continuous arousal-valence dimensions. Contrast with your approach: they
  specify emotions externally and check outputs, while you measure
  naturally emerging internal emotive states with probes and test whether
  the model can self-report them accurately.

7. Zhang and Zhong (2025) — Decoding Emotion in the Deep: A Systematic Study
   of How LLMs Represent, Retain, and Express Emotion
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs develop a well-defined internal geometry of emotion that sharpens
  with model scale and significantly outperforms zero-shot prompting. The
  emotional signal is not a final-layer phenomenon but emerges early and
  peaks mid-network. Internal emotional states are both malleable (can be
  influenced by system prompts) and persistent (initial emotional tone
  remains detectable for hundreds of subsequent tokens).

- Methodology:
  Construct a large-scale Reddit corpus (~400K utterances) balanced across
  seven basic emotions via classification, rewriting, and synthetic
  generation. Train lightweight linear probes to read emotional information
  from hidden layers of Qwen3 and LLaMA models without altering parameters.
  Layer-by-layer analysis of where emotion is encoded. Persistence analysis
  of how long emotional signal endures in the token stream.

- Relevance to my work:
  Extremely relevant — the most directly related paper in this subfolder.
  Like your work, uses linear probes to measure internal emotional
  representations. Their findings about emotion geometry sharpening with
  scale, mid-network peak, and signal persistence all corroborate your
  approach (probing internal concept directions across layers). Their
  persistence finding (emotional tone detectable for hundreds of tokens)
  directly supports your temporal dynamics analysis. The malleable-via-
  prompt finding connects to your steering experiments.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Does not study self-report or probe-report coupling. Does not ask whether
  the model can accurately report its own internal emotive state. No
  numeric self-report extraction. No logit-based estimation. No multi-turn
  naturalistic conversation (uses single-utterance corpus). No activation
  steering (only prompt-based influence). No temporal tracking across
  conversation turns (persistence is measured in tokens, not conversational
  turns). No causal steering interventions. No comparison of internal
  measurement with behavioral output. Focuses on probing alone, not on
  the introspection question.

- Overall takeaway:
  Essential citation for establishing that emotion is geometrically
  structured in LLM representations and detectable via probes. Their
  probe-based findings independently validate your methodological approach.
  Your work extends this by asking the next question: given that we can
  probe emotional states, can the model itself report them accurately?
  This paper provides the representational foundation; yours adds the
  introspective dimension.

8. Wang, Zhang, Yu, Zheng, Gao, Song, Xu, Xia, and Zhang (2025) — Do LLMs
   "Feel"? Emotion Circuits Discovery and Control
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Central finding:
  LLMs contain context-agnostic mechanisms shaping emotional expression.
  Emotion directions reveal consistent, cross-context encoding of emotion.
  Specific neurons and attention heads locally implement emotional
  computation. These components form coherent global emotion circuits.
  Directly modulating these circuits achieves 99.65% emotion-expression
  accuracy, surpassing prompting- and steering-based methods.

- Methodology:
  Construct a controlled dataset (SEV: Scenario-Event with Valence) to
  elicit comparable internal states across emotions. Extract context-
  agnostic emotion directions. Identify emotion-relevant neurons and
  attention heads via analytical decomposition and causal analysis.
  Validate causal roles via ablation and enhancement interventions.
  Quantify sublayer causal influence. Integrate components into global
  emotion circuits. Modulate circuits directly for emotion control.

- Relevance to my work:
  Highly relevant: demonstrates that emotion is encoded in identifiable
  circuits (not just directions), that these circuits are causal (validated
  via ablation), and that modulating them controls emotional expression
  with near-perfect accuracy. Goes beyond linear probing to identify the
  full mechanistic circuitry of emotion. Their causal validation approach
  (ablation, enhancement) parallels your steering experiments:
  both confirm that internal representations causally influence output.
  The emotion direction finding confirms that emotion has a linear
  subspace in activations, consistent with your probe-defined concept
  directions.

- What it lacks relative to my work:
  Focuses on controlling emotional expression in generated text, not on
  whether the model can introspectively report its own internal emotive
  state. No self-report analysis. No numeric self-report extraction. No
  logit-based estimation. No probe-report coupling. No multi-turn
  naturalistic conversation. No temporal dynamics across turns. Controls
  emotion from outside (circuit modulation for output control), whereas
  your work asks whether the model itself has access to its internal
  state. Mechanism identification (circuits) is deeper than probing,
  but the question asked is different: their question is "can we control
  emotion?" while yours is "can the model report its own emotion?"

- Overall takeaway:
  Important citation for mechanistic understanding of emotion in LLMs.
  Establishes that emotion encoding is causal and circuit-level, not
  merely correlational. Complements your work: they find the circuits
  that produce emotional expression, while you test whether the model's
  own report tracks its internal emotive state. Together, the two
  perspectives (mechanistic control and introspective report) bracket the
  question of emotional processing in LLMs from complementary angles.
