WRITING STYLE NOTES — Nicolas Martorell
Extracted from "From Text to Space: Mapping Abstract Spatial Models in LLMs during a Grid-World Navigation Task"
These notes should guide the writing style of the new paper.

================================================================================
1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE
================================================================================

- Favors long, information-dense sentences that pack multiple facts with
  embedded clauses, but keeps them readable by using clear logical connectors.
  
  Example: "By evaluating models of various sizes on a task that requires
  navigating toward a goal, we examine how the format used to encode spatial
  information impacts decision-making."
  
  Example: "Our analysis revealed parameters that significantly predicted the
  position of the agent in the grid, as well as parameters that predicted action
  correctness in the subsequent step, regardless of how spatial information was
  represented in the prompt."

- DO NOT use em-dashes (—, ---). Use parentheses, commas, semicolons, colons,
  or restructured sentences instead. For parenthetical asides, prefer
  parentheses or commas. For elaboration, prefer colons or semicolons.

- Frequently uses "Interestingly," and "Notably," to call attention to
  surprising or important results. This is effective in moderation.

- Tends to front-load motivation before stating results: "To investigate
  whether..., we conducted..." / "With this in mind, we next investigated..."

================================================================================
2. PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE
================================================================================

- Opening paragraphs establish a tension or question, then narrow toward the
  specific contribution. Does NOT start with "In this paper, we..."

  Example intro opening: "Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated
  impressive capabilities in processing and generating text, yet a critical
  question remains: do these models develop abstract internal representations
  of the world, or do they simply memorize typical reasoning paths?"

- Results paragraphs typically: (1) state what was done, (2) report the finding,
  (3) provide statistical evidence inline, (4) interpret what it means.

- Discussion is structured around thematic points, not section-by-section recap.
  Each paragraph in the discussion advances an interpretive argument rather than
  just restating results.

================================================================================
3. STATISTICAL REPORTING
================================================================================

- Reports statistics inline within flowing prose, not in separate tables:
  "(Linear Regression, β = 0.008, p < .001)"
  "(Welch's t-test; Cartesian: t = -3.055, p = 0.004)"
  "(Wilcoxon signed-rank test, W = 16634, p = 0.005)"

- Uses significance language carefully: "marginally significant" for p = 0.053,
  "significantly higher than zero" for p = 0.005.

- Prefers concrete quantitative statements over vague ones:
  "the 8B model achieves a 66% success rate with the JSON SIR but only 30%
  with its best non-cartesian SIR"

================================================================================
4. VOICE AND TONE
================================================================================

- First person plural ("we") throughout. Active voice for experiments:
  "We evaluated...", "We trained...", "We then asked..."

- Passive voice used only when emphasizing the finding over the actor:
  "These correlations might reflect..."

- Confident but measured. Uses hedging when appropriate:
  "These findings point to..." (not "prove")
  "This suggests that..." (not "demonstrates that..." for weaker evidence)
  "It is also interesting that..." for speculative observations.

- Does NOT use overly casual language. No colloquialisms.
  But also not stiff or overly formal — reads as clear, direct scientific prose.

================================================================================
5. FIGURE AND REFERENCE INTEGRATION
================================================================================

- Figures are referenced with explicit panel descriptions:
  "see green curve in left-most panel of Fig. 4"
  "The first three panels of Figure 2 summarize..."

- Cross-references are specific: "(compare same-color curves in the left panel
  of Fig. 2—both JSON and Chess Notation outperform...)"

- Related work citations are woven into sentences, not dumped as lists:
  "Several studies have shown that LLMs can learn and solve tasks in these
  domains [refs], and that their internal representations can be linearly mapped
  to those external structures [ref]."

================================================================================
6. CONTRIBUTIONS FORMAT
================================================================================

- Contributions are a bullet list at the end of the intro, each starting with
  "We demonstrate/show/reveal/find..." — active, discovery-oriented phrasing.

- They are concise (one sentence each) and empirically grounded:
  "We demonstrate that spatial orientation performance scales with model size."
  "We reveal that LLMs can represent the position of an agent in 2D space in
  ways that are partially invariant to prompting."

================================================================================
7. SECTION TRANSITIONS
================================================================================

- Smooth transitions between sections using motivating questions:
  "Spatial reasoning does not only involve representing locations in
  space—it requires detecting relations between objects' positions to make
  predictions and guide decision-making."

- Sections often end with a forward-looking sentence:
  "Based on this observation, we turned to look for abstract components of the
  model that could be representing spatial information irrespective of SIR type."

- Uses "We then wondered if..." / "We next explored whether..." to chain
  analyses into a natural investigative narrative.

================================================================================
8. HEDGING AND CAUTION
================================================================================

- Careful about overclaiming. Acknowledges limitations within results sections,
  not just in a separate limitations section:
  "However, it's important to note that these units may not exclusively process
  spatial information (i.e. they might not be monosemantic)."
  
- Uses "may", "might", "could" for interpretive claims, reserving "demonstrate"
  and "show" for well-supported empirical findings.

- Acknowledges alternative explanations:
  "However, these correlations might reflect the encoding of particular textual
  motifs within a SIR rather than a true, abstract spatial model."

================================================================================
9. DISCUSSION PATTERNS
================================================================================

- Discussion paragraphs are interpretive essays, not result summaries.
  They explain WHY results matter and connect to broader questions.

  Example: "It's interesting, but perhaps expected, that LLMs prefer to process
  spatial information when it's presented mathematically, where the x and y
  dimensions can be operated separately to find the correct orientation."

- Explicitly identifies future directions in concrete terms:
  "In the future, it would be interesting to investigate whether the abstract
  components of the spatial model are necessary when solving harder problems."

- Connects findings across experiments to build a coherent narrative.

================================================================================
10. OVERALL WRITING PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

- Tell a story. Each section leads to the next through a question-answer chain.
- Be precise with numbers and statistical claims.
- Use active voice. First person plural. Direct statements.
- Front-load context, then narrow to specific result.
- Moderate use of emphasis words (Interestingly, Notably, Crucially).
- Acknowledge limitations honestly, within the text, not just at the end.
- Keep related work functional (what does each cited work do for YOUR argument?).
- Make contributions clear, concise, and empirically grounded.
