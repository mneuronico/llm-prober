### 2.1 Introspection and self-report in language models

A growing literature asks whether language models can report anything meaningful about their own epistemic or internal state. Early evidence suggested that models can often estimate whether their answers are correct and, to some extent, whether they know an answer at all. Kadavath et al. showed that language models can provide useful self-evaluations of answer correctness through measures such as (P(\mathrm{True})) and (P(\mathrm{IK})), while Yin et al. studied self-knowledge more directly through answerable and unanswerable questions, finding that LLMs possess some capacity to recognize the limits of their knowledge. A related line of work studies how such self-assessments can be elicited and calibrated. Tian et al. found that confidence scores elicited directly from instruction-tuned models can be better calibrated than naïvely using conditional probabilities, while Ren et al. showed that self-evaluation signals can improve selective generation in open-ended tasks. Kumar et al. further connected expressed confidence to internal token-probability structure, introducing the notion of confidence-probability alignment, and Yona et al. argued that models often fail to faithfully express their intrinsic uncertainty in natural language even when internal uncertainty is present. Together, these papers suggest that self-reports can be informative, but that the relationship between internal signal and expressed report is fragile and method-dependent. ([ACL Anthology][17])

More recent work has begun to treat self-evaluation not only as a diagnostic but also as a target for intervention. Kapoor et al. argued that large language models do not automatically “know what they don’t know” in a sufficiently calibrated way and that such uncertainty awareness often must be explicitly taught. In a related vein, Zhang et al. used self-evaluation as a training signal to improve factuality, showing that self-assessment can be behaviorally useful for alignment. Binder et al. push the strongest version of this idea, arguing that models can sometimes learn about themselves by introspection in the sense of exploiting privileged access to their own behavioral tendencies. ([OpenReview][5])

At the same time, the literature remains unsettled. Song et al. challenge strong introspection claims by showing that, in linguistic domains where a model’s knowledge can be grounded in its own string probabilities, prompted metalinguistic judgments do not provide evidence of privileged self-access beyond what can be predicted from matched comparison models. This tension is important: current work supports the idea that LLM self-reports can be useful and sometimes calibrated, but it does not yet establish when such reports genuinely track internal state rather than reflecting prompt-sensitive verbal behavior. ([arXiv][16])

Our work builds on this literature but differs from it in three ways. First, we focus on **numeric self-reports of ongoing internal state**, rather than only correctness, abstention, or factual uncertainty. Second, we study these reports in **multi-turn conversational settings**, where internal state can evolve over time. Third, we define introspection operationally through the relationship between self-report and an independently measured probe-defined internal direction, allowing us to ask not only whether models can self-report, but whether those reports track internal state descriptively, causally, and through time.

### 2.2 Probing internal representations and behavioral concept directions

A central methodological question in this work is whether a model’s hidden states contain linearly accessible information about high-level variables of interest. Probing methods address this question by training simple readouts on internal representations. Early work by Alain and Bengio introduced linear classifier probes as a way to diagnose what information is available at intermediate layers, and subsequent work showed that appropriately designed probes can recover structured latent properties rather than only flat labels. In particular, Hewitt and Manning demonstrated that syntax is organized geometrically in contextual word representations, while Belinkov’s review summarizes how probing became a standard tool for studying linguistic and semantic structure in neural models. ([arXiv][1])

At the same time, the probing literature has emphasized that probe performance is not self-interpreting. Hewitt and Liang showed that high probe accuracy may reflect probe capacity rather than information genuinely encoded in the representation, introducing control tasks and selectivity as a corrective. Pimentel et al. further argued that conclusions drawn from probes depend on the probe family and evaluation criterion, cautioning against overly simple readings of probe scores as direct evidence of representation content. These concerns are directly relevant here: we do not treat successful probing alone as sufficient evidence that a concept direction is valid, but instead combine held-out probe validation with report-side analyses, random-vector controls, and causal steering interventions. ([ACL Anthology][2])

Our use of contrastively trained internal directions is also closely related to the broader literature on concept vectors. Kim et al.’s TCAV introduced the influential idea that human-interpretable concepts can correspond to directions in activation space. More recently, this idea has been extended to language models and generalized into the broader paradigm of representation engineering, which treats population-level directions in activation space as objects that can both reveal and manipulate high-level model properties. In this framing, internal directions are not merely descriptive summaries, but candidate handles for intervention. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][6])

Several recent LLM papers are especially relevant to our setting. Burns et al. showed that latent knowledge can sometimes be extracted from hidden states even when it is not faithfully expressed in the model’s default outputs. Azaria and Mitchell found that internal activations can reveal whether a model is producing truthful or false statements, and Gurnee and Tegmark showed that large language models linearly represent abstract variables such as space and time. Together, these results support the broader premise that hidden states can encode behaviorally meaningful variables in a relatively accessible form. ([arXiv][7])

Finally, our work is closely connected to activation steering. Turner et al. showed that adding contrastive activation differences can steer model outputs at inference time, and Panickssery et al. extended this idea with contrastive activation addition computed over behavioral examples. Arditi et al. provide particularly strong causal evidence for the importance of internal directions, showing that refusal behavior in chat models can be added or removed by intervening on a low-dimensional direction. We build on this line of work by using concept directions not only to steer behavior, but to test whether a model’s numeric self-reports covary with and causally depend on independently measured internal-state directions. ([arXiv][11])

### 2.3 Activation steering and causal interventions

A growing line of work studies whether high-level model behavior can be changed by intervening directly on internal representations at inference time. Turner et al. introduced activation engineering as a general framework for modifying activations to steer model outputs, and proposed Activation Addition (ActAdd), in which steering vectors are constructed from contrastive prompt activations and injected during generation. In parallel, Li et al. introduced Inference-Time Intervention (ITI), showing that targeted interventions on learned internal directions can substantially improve truthfulness. Together, these works established that inference-time internal interventions can be both lightweight and behaviorally powerful. ([arXiv][2])

This interventionist perspective has since been generalized under the broader paradigm of representation engineering. Zou et al. argue that population-level representations can serve as tractable objects for both reading and controlling high-level properties such as honesty, harmlessness, and power-seeking. In this view, internal directions are not merely descriptive summaries of model activity, but candidate causal handles on behavior. ([arXiv][5])

Several steering methods are especially relevant to our setting because they use contrastive examples to define the intervention direction. Panickssery et al. propose Contrastive Activation Addition, which estimates steering vectors by averaging activation differences between positive and negative behavioral examples. This is particularly close to our setup, where concept directions are trained from contrastive behavioral data rather than hand-crafted prompt pairs alone. More broadly, work on style vectors shows that activation-based interventions can also steer sentiment, emotion, and related high-level attributes in generative language models. ([arXiv][6])

Recent work has also strengthened the causal interpretation of low-dimensional steering directions. Arditi et al. show that refusal behavior in chat models is strongly mediated by a single direction: adding that direction induces refusal, while ablating it removes refusal behavior. Results of this kind are important for our purposes because they justify using directional intervention as more than a control method: steering can serve as causal evidence that a measured internal variable is functionally implicated in a downstream behavior. ([NeurIPS Proceedings][7])

Our work builds directly on this literature, but uses steering for a different purpose. Rather than mainly asking whether a direction can control overt behavior such as refusal, toxicity, or truthfulness, we ask whether numeric self-reports about internal state depend causally on independently measured concept directions. This lets us use steering as a test of introspection itself: if a probe-defined internal direction is genuinely related to a model’s report of that state, then intervening on that direction should shift the report in the predicted direction. We further extend this logic by asking whether steering one concept can improve introspection for another, treating representation-level intervention not only as a control tool but as a method for modulating introspective capacity. ([arXiv][2])

### 2.4 Temporal dynamics and conversation-induced state drift

Recent work suggests that large language models should not be treated as behaviorally static over the course of a dialogue. Li et al. show that system-prompted chatbots exhibit substantial **instruction instability** in multi-turn self-chat, with behavior drifting away from the initial instruction within only a few rounds of interaction. Their analysis further suggests that this is not merely a superficial prompting artifact, but is linked to the way attention over earlier context decays during extended exchanges. ([arXiv][1])

A related line of work studies drift in more identity- or persona-like properties. Lee et al. examine **identity drift** in conversations of LLM agents across multiple themes and models, while Abdulhai et al. show that off-the-shelf LLMs frequently drift from assigned personas, contradict earlier statements, or abandon role-appropriate behavior over multi-turn interactions. Earlier dialogue work had already identified persona consistency across turns as a major challenge, but recent LLM studies make clear that such instability remains a central issue even in modern instruction-tuned systems. ([arXiv][3])

Temporal structure matters not only because instructions or personas can drift, but also because models are sensitive to **where** information appears in a sequence. Guo et al. show that LLMs exhibit serial-position effects, including primacy- and recency-like biases, indicating that position within context can systematically shape model outputs. This is methodologically important for our setting: if both internal measurements and self-reports evolve with turn number, then pooled correlations may partially reflect shared temporal structure rather than genuine coupling between the two. ([arXiv][2])

Our work builds on this literature by shifting the focus from drift in externally visible behaviors, such as instruction-following or persona consistency, to drift in **probe-defined internal concept state** and in the model’s **numeric self-reports** of that state. This motivates two design choices in our experiments: first, we analyze introspection turn by turn rather than only in aggregate; second, we explicitly test whether probe-report coupling is already present at the first turn and how it changes over time. ([arXiv][1])

### 2.5 Calibration, confidence estimation, and numeric reporting

A closely related literature studies how to extract reliable confidence signals from language models. Early work showed that models can sometimes produce useful explicit self-evaluations, such as the probability that a proposed answer is correct or the probability that the model knows the answer at all. Kadavath et al. introduced this framing through elicited probabilities such as (P(\mathrm{True})) and (P(\mathrm{IK})), establishing that confidence can be treated as an output to be queried rather than only inferred from next-token likelihoods. More broadly, work on linguistic calibration showed that the way a model verbally expresses certainty or doubt is itself a measurable object, and that this expression is often misaligned with actual correctness unless specifically improved. ([arXiv][1])

Subsequent work emphasized that **how** confidence is elicited matters. Tian et al. showed that for aligned LLMs, verbalized confidence scores can be better calibrated than raw conditional probabilities, while Xiong et al. provided a systematic study of confidence elicitation methods, showing that prompting, sampling, and aggregation choices all substantially affect the quality of the resulting confidence estimates. These results are especially relevant for our setting because they imply that a naïve readout of the final generated token may be a poor proxy for the model’s underlying graded belief state. ([arXiv][3])

Recent work has also focused more explicitly on the relationship between internal probability structure and expressed confidence. Kumar et al. introduce the notion of **confidence-probability alignment**, connecting a model’s internal confidence, quantified through token probabilities, to the confidence it verbalizes when explicitly asked. Yona et al. similarly argue that LLMs often fail to faithfully express their intrinsic uncertainty in words, highlighting a systematic gap between latent uncertainty and surface report. Together, these papers suggest that the discrete emitted confidence token is only one possible readout of a richer internal probability distribution, and that faithful uncertainty expression is itself a nontrivial modeling problem. ([ACL Anthology][5])

A parallel line of work uses token-level distributions more directly. Entropy-based methods, including semantic-entropy approaches, show that uncertainty signals often become visible when one examines the broader predictive distribution rather than only the selected output string. In particular, Farquhar et al. show that entropy-based estimators can detect hallucination-prone generations that would be hard to diagnose from the final response alone. More generally, survey and comparison work has grouped existing estimators into elicited-confidence, token-probability, consistency-based, and hidden-state-based families, making clear that no single surface readout fully captures model confidence in all settings. ([Nature][8])

Our use of logit-based numeric self-reports is adjacent to, but distinct from, this literature. Confidence-calibration work usually asks how likely a model is to be **correct**. By contrast, we ask what numeric value a model assigns to a **self-report about an internal concept state**. The methodological lesson we borrow is that distributional information in the logits can be more informative than the final decoded token. This motivates our probability-weighted averaging over the digit-token logits: rather than treating the emitted integer as the only report, we treat the local logit distribution over digits as a richer and less collapsed estimate of the model’s latent numeric self-assessment. ([ACL Anthology][5])

### 2.6 AI safety, model welfare, and machine psychology

Beyond interpretability, there are ethical reasons to care whether AI self-reports carry genuine information about internal state. Perez and Long argue that, under the right conditions, self-reports could become one source of evidence in debates about whether AI systems have states of possible moral significance. This connects to a broader emerging literature on AI welfare. Long argues that AI welfare and moral patienthood may become practically relevant in the near future and that AI developers should begin assessing systems for indicators of consciousness, robust agency, and related morally significant capacities. Our work does not attempt to determine whether current LLMs are conscious or welfare-bearing. Instead, it contributes a narrower methodological tool: a way to evaluate whether numeric self-reports about internal state are informative, stable, and causally connected to independently measured internal variables. ([arXiv][2])

Our approach also fits within the broader empirical study of AI behavior. Rahwan et al. call for a science of machine behaviour that treats AI systems as objects of empirical investigation, while Hagendorff frames the study of LLM traits and tendencies as an emerging “machine psychology.” In this spirit, introspective reporting can be studied as one behavioral capacity among others, with the important caveat that human psychological vocabulary should not be imported uncritically. ## Draft of subsection 2.7

### 2.7 Neuroscience and psychology as methodological analogies

A useful methodological analogy comes from neuroscience and psychology, where subjective report is routinely used as one source of evidence about internal state. In metacognition research, participants are often asked to provide confidence or awareness judgments, and these reports are evaluated by how well they track objective performance or experimentally manipulated evidence. This literature treats introspective report as neither infallible nor useless, but as a graded and quantifiable signal whose informativeness can be measured. ([annualreviews.org][1])

This is especially relevant for our operational framing. Human metacognition is commonly measured not by the mere existence of a confidence report, but by the degree to which that report covaries with success or evidence on a trial-by-trial basis. Formal tools such as meta-d′ were developed precisely to separate first-order task performance from second-order report quality. In this respect, our use of probe–report coupling as an operational measure of introspection is closer in spirit to metacognitive sensitivity analysis than to a binary notion of “having” or “lacking” introspection. ([PubMed][3])

A second useful lesson is that report method matters. Work on perceptual awareness and confidence shows that subjective and objective measures may converge or diverge depending on task design, and that even closely related subjective scales such as visibility and confidence are not interchangeable. More generally, different methods of report can reveal different kinds of access to an underlying internal state. This provides a principled analogy for our comparison between greedy integer outputs, sampled outputs, and logit-based numeric reports: different readouts need not be equally sensitive to the same latent variable. ([PMC][5])

Finally, neuroscience also motivates decomposing introspection into multiple components. Contemporary work distinguishes metacognitive monitoring from metacognitive control and identifies neural correlates of report quality that are partly separable from first-order task performance. We draw on this distinction only as an analogy, not as a claim of equivalence between humans and language models. The point is methodological: in both domains, it is useful to distinguish whether an internal state is well formed and whether the system can report that state in a way that preserves information about it. ([PMC][9])
